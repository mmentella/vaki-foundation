# Hypothesis and Experiment Model

## 1. Purpose

This document defines how VAKI understands hypotheses and experiments as domain-independent reasoning structures.

Its purpose is to make provisional claims and deliberate reality-facing tests inspectable and challengeable without turning inquiry into a mandatory scientific-method pipeline.

This model owns:

- Hypothesis semantics;
- Experiment semantics;
- semantic relationships between Hypothesis and Experiment;
- limited boundary language for expected observable consequences.

It does not own Question, Inquiry, Observation, Observation Context, Observation provenance, Evidence, evidential interpretation, Evidence Strength, Observation Quality, Uncertainty, Decision, Decision Reasoning, Commitment, Learning Value, Problem Framing, Explanation as a full canonical concept, global Model semantics, Prediction as a full canonical concept, validation protocols, prototype artifact semantics, Feedback semantics, or Learning semantics.

This model is not a research workflow, scientific method, lifecycle, state machine, validation process, experiment registry, hypothesis tracking system, statistical methodology, approval process, schema, database model, graph model, or tooling specification.

The Foundation defines shared meaning. Manifestations inherit, interpret, implement, and may extend locally without silently redefining canonical semantics.

## 2. Position in the Foundation

The VAKI Thinking Model describes a broad reasoning topology that includes observation, framing, modeling, hypothesis formation, experimentation, evidence interpretation, decision, manifestation, sharing, and feedback.

This model refines the narrow semantic layer where provisional claims and deliberate reality-facing test arrangements interact with that topology.

Conceptually, it sits among existing models as:

```text
Questions and Inquiry Model
  <-> Hypothesis and Experiment Model
  <-> Observation Model
  <-> Evidence Model
```

Decision reasoning and design methodology interact with this layer according to their existing ownership.

This is not an execution order. A Question may arise from Observation. Existing Evidence may challenge a Hypothesis without a new Experiment. A Decision may create the need for an Experiment. An Experiment may generate unexpected Observations and new Questions. Inquiry may remain exploratory without Hypothesis formation.

The purpose of the topology is semantic clarity, not required progression through stages.

## 3. Hypothesis

A Hypothesis is a provisional and challengeable claim about reality, a relation within reality, or the expected effect of an intervention.

A Hypothesis may concern, for example:

- structure;
- behavior;
- causal relation;
- descriptive regularity;
- mechanism;
- expected effect of an intervention.

This list is illustrative. It does not create a mandatory taxonomy.

A Hypothesis is provisional because it remains open to revision, qualification, weakening, rejection, or replacement when relevant Observation and Evidence warrant that change.

A Hypothesis is challengeable because it can be brought into possible relation with reality-facing Observation and Evidence. Challengeability does not require strict philosophical falsifiability, a formal prediction, a controlled experiment, or a universal method. It means the claim is not protected from evidence by definition.

A Hypothesis is not automatically true because it is plausible, useful, elegant, preferred, generated by a model, consistent with current understanding, or temporarily useful for reasoning.

Evidence may support, weaken, contradict, qualify, leave unresolved, or invalidate a Hypothesis when warranted. A single observation does not automatically determine any of these relations. The Evidence Model owns evidential interpretation, strength, conflict, body-of-evidence reasoning, and uncertainty.

## 4. Hypothesis and Question

A Question asks or identifies insufficient understanding. A Hypothesis provisionally claims.

A Question may exist without a Hypothesis. Inquiry may proceed by observation, inspection, comparison, modeling, synthesis, simulation, conversation, or exploration before any formal Hypothesis is useful.

A Hypothesis may relate to one or more Questions. Multiple Hypotheses may bear on one Question. A Hypothesis may generate new Questions by exposing assumptions, expected consequences, contradictions, or boundary conditions.

The relation between Question and Hypothesis is many-to-many and revisable:

- a Question may generate a Hypothesis;
- a Hypothesis may clarify, narrow, or reframe a Question;
- rejection or weakening of a Hypothesis does not necessarily resolve the originating Question;
- resolution of a Question does not automatically validate a Hypothesis;
- a Hypothesis is not an Answer merely because it is plausible, preferred, or currently unchallenged.

Question and Inquiry semantics remain owned by the Questions and Inquiry Model.

## 5. Hypothesis and Assumption

An Assumption is held or accepted as a condition for current reasoning, analysis, design, or action.

A Hypothesis is exposed as a provisional claim whose relation to Observation and Evidence may be investigated.

The boundary is practical:

- an Assumption may later become the subject of Inquiry;
- an Assumption may be reformulated as a Hypothesis;
- a Hypothesis may be temporarily used as a working assumption;
- not every Assumption must become a Hypothesis;
- not every Assumption must be experimentally tested.

Making an Assumption explicit does not mean it has been tested. Treating a Hypothesis as a working assumption does not make it true, resolved, or decision-sufficient.

This model does not create an Assumption lifecycle, Assumption Model, assumption registry, or required testing procedure.

## 6. Hypothesis, Explanation, and Model

Explanation is boundary language in this model, not a primary owned concept.

A Hypothesis may be explanatory, but need not be. It may claim that a condition holds, a relation exists, a pattern recurs, an intervention has an expected effect, or a mechanism is responsible for an outcome.

An Explanation may contain or depend on Hypotheses. Explanatory usefulness is distinct from evidential strength. A useful Explanation can remain weakly evidenced, partially supported, contradicted in some conditions, or dependent on assumptions. Plausibility is not Evidence.

Revising an Explanation does not rewrite the underlying Observations. Observation correction belongs to the Observation Model. Evidential interpretation belongs to the Evidence Model.

This model does not define a taxonomy of explanations, explanatory scores, explanatory quality, or a full canonical Explanation concept.

This model also does not define Model globally.

Hypotheses may be derived from, embedded in, challenge, compare, or revise Models. Models may generate expected observable consequences. A Model may remain useful even when one Hypothesis derived from it is weakened, and a Hypothesis may be supported without validating the whole Model from which it came.

The truth, credibility, or usefulness of a Model must not be collapsed into the status of a single Hypothesis. M1.5 does not own global Model semantics, domain-specific model methodology, model selection, or a generic Model ontology.

## 7. Expected Observable Consequences

A Hypothesis or Model may imply an expected observable consequence.

An expected observable consequence is a lightweight semantic relation between a claim or model and something that might be observed under relevant conditions. It is not a full Prediction ontology.

Expected observable consequences may concern:

- future events;
- current conditions;
- historical traces;
- absence;
- presence;
- pattern;
- response;
- change.

Not every Hypothesis must produce a formal Prediction. Not every Experiment requires a formal Prediction.

Failure to observe an expected observable consequence does not automatically falsify a Hypothesis. It may weaken, qualify, contradict, leave unresolved, or fail to bear on the Hypothesis depending on observation conditions, detection limits, alternative explanations, assumptions, and the body of evidence.

The Observation Model owns the distinction between absence of observation and observation of absence. The Evidence Model owns whether an observed absence or non-observation bears evidentially on a Hypothesis, Model, Question, uncertainty, or decision.

An expected observable consequence is therefore not automatic proof, automatic falsification, or automatic Evidence.

## 8. Experiment

An Experiment is a deliberate reality-facing inquiry arrangement, intervention, or controlled interaction intended to create conditions under which relevant Observations may become possible.

An Experiment creates, changes, selects, or arranges conditions of interaction with reality. It does not own Observation. The Observation Model owns what becomes an Observation.

An Experiment may:

- address a Question;
- challenge or investigate a Hypothesis;
- explore without a prior formal Hypothesis;
- produce expected or unexpected Observations;
- create conditions for comparison;
- involve intervention, control, instrumentation, operation, artifact use, or structured interaction;
- use existing Observations and Evidence without requiring a new reality-facing intervention.

Experiments may be comparative, exploratory, intervention-based, simulation-assisted, artifact-based, or otherwise locally designed. These are examples, not a mandatory taxonomy.

Not every Experiment requires a formal Prediction. Not every Hypothesis requires an Experiment. Existing Observations and Evidence may bear on a Hypothesis without a new Experiment.

Simulation requires care. A simulation may participate in Inquiry or Experiment design, and it may be part of an experimental arrangement when its relation to reality, inputs, assumptions, and limits are understood. A simulation alone does not automatically count as interaction with external reality. Its output becomes evidential only according to the relevant Observation and Evidence boundaries and the relation between the simulation, its assumptions, and the reality under inquiry.

## 9. Experiment and Inquiry

Inquiry is the directed and revisable pursuit of better understanding in relation to one or more Questions. This model does not redefine Inquiry.

Inquiry may include Hypothesis formation. Inquiry may include Experiment. Inquiry need not include either. Inquiry may remain exploratory.

An Experiment is one possible inquiry activity. It is not the container for all inquiry, and inquiry is not required to become experimental in order to be legitimate.

Experiments may generate new Questions. Hypotheses may generate new Questions. Inquiry may recursively revise Questions, Hypotheses, experimental arrangements, and directions of investigation.

This model does not create an Inquiry workflow, research lifecycle, experiment backlog, or required path from Question to Hypothesis to Experiment.

## 10. Experiment and Observation

Experiment concerns deliberate arrangement or intervention.

Observation concerns what is selected, mediated, bounded, preserved, and represented from interaction with reality.

An Experiment may create or arrange conditions in which something can be perceived, measured, experienced, detected, or recorded. That does not make the Experiment an Observation. Interaction with reality may produce effects that are not observed, not preserved, not relevant, or not interpretable.

Experimental conditions may be relevant Observation Context. Intervention origin may be relevant to provenance where necessary to understand the Observation.

The Observation Model owns:

- what it means to become an Observation;
- Observation Context;
- selection and boundaries;
- measurement boundary;
- preservation;
- provenance semantics;
- absence of observation and observation of absence;
- observation correction;
- Observation Quality;
- the boundary between feedback and observation.

M1.5 references these boundaries only to keep Experiment from absorbing Observation.

## 11. Experiment Result and Evidence

An experiment result is not automatically Evidence.

The common epistemic layering is:

```text
Experiment
  -> conditions for interaction
  -> possible Observation
  -> possible evidential relation
  -> changed or unchanged state of uncertainty
```

This is not a mandatory workflow. It names distinctions that must remain visible.

An Experiment may fail to produce relevant Observation. It may produce Observations that are ambiguous, noisy, incomplete, unexpected, or irrelevant to the intended Hypothesis. It may produce information that bears on a different Question than the one originally pursued.

The Evidence Model owns whether information bears on a claim, Question, Hypothesis, Model, Explanation, uncertainty, or Decision. It also owns support, contradiction, qualification, inconclusive relation, evidential strength, conflicting evidence, interpretation, and body-of-evidence reasoning.

This model does not define evidence scores, experimental success criteria, acceptance thresholds, statistical rules, or decision sufficiency.

## 12. Experiment, Prototype, and Validation

Prototype semantics are owned by the Design Methodology and related Foundation usage. This model uses only boundary language.

A Prototype is an artifact or deliberately incomplete manifestation. An Experiment is an inquiry arrangement, intervention, or deliberate reality-facing test structure.

A Prototype may participate in an Experiment. An Experiment may use no Prototype. A Prototype may be used for learning, exploration, demonstration, validation, or design iteration without every use being an Experiment. An Experiment does not necessarily produce a Prototype.

Equating Prototype and Experiment hides the difference between artifact and inquiry arrangement.

Validation is also distinct from Experiment.

An Experiment may contribute to Validation. Validation may use experimental and non-experimental evidence. Experiment and Validation are not synonyms.

M1.5 does not prescribe validation protocols, acceptance criteria, certification rules, approval processes, or a Validation Model.

## 13. Hypothesis, Experiment, and Decision

Decision semantics are owned by the Decision Model.

A Decision may proceed under unresolved Hypotheses. Unresolved explanatory uncertainty does not prevent Commitment by definition. A Decision is a justified commitment under current evidence, uncertainty, consequence, timing, and reversibility, not proof that all relevant Hypotheses are resolved.

Deciding to run an Experiment is a Decision when it commits resources, direction, time, constraints, or options. The Experiment itself is not the Decision.

Acting as though a Hypothesis were true may become decision-relevant when it constrains action or creates Commitment. Provisional use of a working Hypothesis is not automatically Commitment.

The Decision Model owns whether available Evidence and uncertainty are sufficient for Commitment. This model does not create decision thresholds, hypothesis confidence requirements, or experiment-based approval gates.

## 14. Revision and Inconclusive Outcomes

Hypotheses are revisable.

A Hypothesis may be retained, revised, narrowed, qualified, weakened, rejected, or replaced. These are possible reasoning outcomes, not lifecycle states.

This model does not define transition rules, statuses, registries, schemas, approval gates, confidence scores, or required review steps.

Evidence may fail to discriminate between competing Hypotheses. Multiple Hypotheses may remain viable. Rejection of one Hypothesis does not imply acceptance of another. Inconclusive Inquiry is legitimate.

Unexpected Observation may generate a new Hypothesis instead of merely accepting or rejecting an existing one. A Hypothesis may also be left unresolved when current Observation and Evidence do not responsibly support, weaken, contradict, or qualify it.

Revision of a Hypothesis does not automatically revise the Question, Observation, Evidence, Model, Explanation, Decision, or Commitment connected to it. Those changes may interact, but their semantics remain owned by their respective models.

## 15. Negative Knowledge

Investigation of a Hypothesis or Experiment may produce durable knowledge even when the preferred claim is weakened, the intended result does not appear, or the inquiry remains inconclusive.

Such knowledge may concern:

- failed assumptions;
- invalid conditions;
- non-working interventions;
- boundary conditions;
- missing discriminating evidence;
- unresolved alternatives.

This is consistent with the Foundation principle to preserve negative knowledge and with the Evidence Model's ownership of Negative Knowledge semantics.

M1.5 does not create a Negative Knowledge taxonomy or absorb ownership from the Evidence Model. The point is narrower: unsuccessful or inconclusive experiments may still produce useful knowledge when their context and evidential meaning are preserved.

## 16. Non-Pipeline Semantics

Hypothesis and Experiment do not create a universal sequence such as:

```text
Problem
  -> Question
  -> Hypothesis
  -> Prediction
  -> Experiment
  -> Observation
  -> Evidence
  -> Decision
```

These concepts form semantic relations, not a mandatory workflow.

Relations may be optional, recursive, many-to-many, and non-sequential.

Examples include:

- Observation generating a Question;
- existing Evidence challenging a Hypothesis without a new Experiment;
- Experiment generating unexpected Observation and new Questions;
- Decision creating the need for an Experiment;
- Hypothesis arising from contradiction between existing Observations;
- Inquiry remaining exploratory without Hypothesis formation.

The Foundation distinguishes these concepts so manifestations can reason clearly, not so every manifestation must traverse them in the same order.

## 17. Manifestation Autonomy

Manifestations remain free to choose local methods and implementation details.

This includes, but is not limited to:

- hypothesis generation methods;
- research methodology;
- experiment design;
- statistical approaches;
- Bayesian methods;
- frequentist methods;
- causal inference methods;
- simulation methods;
- machine learning methods;
- testing tools;
- model selection;
- experiment tracking;
- hypothesis registries;
- confidence scoring;
- validation protocols;
- peer review;
- approval processes;
- data schemas;
- storage;
- traceability mechanisms;
- automation;
- governance.

The Foundation defines semantic boundaries, not mandatory operational mechanisms.

A manifestation may extend Hypothesis or Experiment concepts locally when its domain requires additional distinctions, methods, records, tools, or governance. Those extensions should remain distinguishable from Foundation meaning.

A manifestation may not silently redefine the Foundation meaning of Hypothesis, Experiment, Observation, Evidence, Inquiry, Decision, Prototype, or Validation while claiming continuity with this model.

## 18. Failure Modes

Common failure modes include:

- treating a Question as though it already contained a Hypothesis;
- treating an Assumption as tested merely because it is explicit;
- requiring a Hypothesis before every Inquiry;
- requiring an Experiment for every Hypothesis;
- requiring a Prediction for every Experiment;
- treating an experiment result as automatically Evidence;
- treating one unexpected result as automatic falsification;
- treating absence of Observation as Observation of absence;
- equating Prototype and Experiment;
- equating Experiment and Validation;
- allowing Explanation plausibility to substitute for Evidence;
- turning Hypothesis revision outcomes into a lifecycle state machine;
- treating this model as a universal scientific method pipeline;
- collapsing the status of a Model into the status of a single Hypothesis;
- treating simulation output as evidential without preserving its relation to reality, inputs, assumptions, and limits;
- treating a Decision made under unresolved Hypotheses as proof that the Hypotheses are resolved.

These failure modes are not governance controls, checklist items, approval gates, or workflow requirements. They are recurring ways reasoning can lose semantic clarity.

## 19. Invariants

Across VAKI manifestations:

1. Question is not Hypothesis.
2. Assumption is not automatically Hypothesis.
3. Hypothesis is provisional and challengeable.
4. Hypothesis does not require Experiment.
5. Experiment does not require formal Hypothesis.
6. Experiment does not own Observation.
7. Experiment result is not automatically Evidence.
8. Expected observable consequence is not automatic proof or falsification.
9. Evidence semantics remain Evidence-owned.
10. Decision semantics remain Decision-owned.
11. Prototype is not Experiment.
12. Inquiry is not required to follow a fixed sequence.
13. Manifestations retain methodological autonomy.
14. Plausibility is not Evidence.
15. Rejection of one Hypothesis does not imply acceptance of another.
16. Observation absence and Evidence of absence must remain distinct according to Observation and Evidence semantics.

## 20. Relationship to Existing Documents

This model refines Hypothesis and Experiment semantics used across the Foundation without replacing existing ownership.

The [VAKI Thinking Model](README.md) owns the broad reasoning topology, including Problem Framing, Modeling, Hypothesis, Experiment, Decision, Manifestation, Share, and Feedback topology. This model narrows Hypothesis and Experiment semantics within that topology and rejects their treatment as mandatory pipeline stages.

The [Questions and Inquiry Model](QUESTIONS_AND_INQUIRY_MODEL.md) owns Question, Inquiry, Inquiry Context, Question emergence, Question evolution, Answer, Resolution, Suspension, Reopening, Recursive Inquiry, Question Quality, and Inquiry Traceability. This model defines how Hypotheses and Experiments may relate to Questions and Inquiry without redefining them.

The [Observation Model](OBSERVATION_MODEL.md) owns Observation, Observation Context, selection, preservation, provenance origin, measurement boundary, correction, absence/non-observation, Observation Quality, and the feedback-to-observation boundary. This model defines Experiment as an arrangement or intervention that may create conditions for Observation, while leaving Observation semantics to that model.

The [Evidence Model](EVIDENCE.md) owns Signal, Evidence, evidential interpretation, Evidence Quality, Evidence Strength, Body of Evidence, Uncertainty, Negative Knowledge, contextual validity, and evidential traceability. This model defines Hypothesis and Experiment as possible objects or sources in evidence reasoning without redefining Evidence.

The [Decision Model](../method/DECISION_MODEL.md) owns Decision Situation, Decision Object, Decision Reasoning, Commitment, Reversibility, Optionality, Cost of Delay, Learning Value, decision quality, and decision traceability. This model defines how Hypotheses and Experiments may become decision-relevant without defining decision sufficiency.

The [Design Methodology](../method/DESIGN_METHODOLOGY.md) owns domain-independent design guidance, including the use of prototypes, experiments, evidence, and decisions in system evolution. This model preserves the boundary between Prototype and Experiment without rewriting design methodology as scientific method.

The [Foundation Specification](../FOUNDATION_SPEC.md) and [ADR-0001](../../decisions/ADR-0001-foundation-manifestation-boundary.md) own the Foundation-to-manifestation relationship. This model respects that boundary by defining shared meaning while leaving domain-specific research methods, experiment practices, tools, records, schemas, statistics, validation protocols, governance, and automation to manifestations.
