# VAKI Observation Model

## 1. Purpose

This document defines what it means for VAKI to observe something about reality before that observation becomes signal, evidence, knowledge, or decision input.

The Observation Model is the primary semantic owner of:

- observation;
- observation context;
- selection in observation;
- perception, measurement, experience, and detection as possible sources;
- preservation;
- provenance origin;
- observation correction;
- observation completeness limits;
- the boundary between observation and interpretation;
- the boundary between feedback and observation;
- the boundary between absence of observation and observation of absence;
- the relationship between observation and measurement.

It does not define evidence strength, body of evidence, state of uncertainty, decision reasoning, commitment, reversibility, optionality, or manifestation. Those concepts belong to the Evidence Model, Decision Model, Thinking Model, or other Foundation documents.

The purpose is not to prescribe observation tools, logging systems, sensors, databases, schemas, templates, or procedures. The purpose is to preserve the semantic boundary between interaction with reality and evidence.

## 2. Position in the Thinking Model

In the VAKI Thinking Model, observation sits after interaction with reality and before evidence-facing interpretation:

```text
Reality
  -> interaction with reality
  -> perception, measurement, experience, or detection
  -> Observation
  -> possible Signal
  -> Evidence relation
  -> changed state of uncertainty
  -> Decision Reasoning
  -> Commitment
  -> Manifestation
  -> further interaction with reality
  -> Feedback
  -> Observation
```

This is not a mandatory pipeline. Observation can lead to reframing, new measurement, reinterpretation, correction, model revision, hypothesis revision, experiment design, decision revision, or renewed interaction with reality.

The Observation Model defines what happens semantically between interaction with reality and evidence. It gives VAKI a stable answer to this question:

> Before something can become evidence, what does it mean to have observed something about reality?

## 3. Interaction with Reality

Reality is the external reference that constrains VAKI reasoning.

Interaction with reality is any contact through which reality may produce effects that can be perceived, measured, experienced, detected, or recorded. It may occur through direct perception, deliberate experiment, operation, use, failure, physical trace, system behavior, human report, instrument output, environmental condition, market behavior, or another reality-facing encounter.

Interaction with reality is not yet observation. Reality may produce effects that are not noticed, selected, recorded, preserved, or made available to reasoning.

Observation begins when something from such an interaction is selected and preserved with enough context to be inspectable as an account of what was perceived, measured, experienced, or detected.

## 4. Observation

An observation is a reality-constrained, selective, contextual, and preservable account of something perceived, measured, experienced, or detected through interaction with reality, before stronger claims about meaning are added.

An observation is not automatically:

- true;
- complete;
- objective;
- evidence;
- signal;
- explanation;
- claim;
- conclusion.

An observation may be incomplete, noisy, distorted, context-bound, instrument-mediated, observer-mediated, corrected later, superseded by a better preserved account, or irrelevant to later reasoning.

Observation is not interpretation-free reality. Selection, attention, instrumentation, prior framing, categories, and recording conditions may shape what becomes observable and how it is described.

Observation is also not unrestricted interpretation. It remains constrained by something perceived, measured, experienced, or detected in relation to reality. As far as practicable, what was observed should remain distinguishable from what was inferred from it.

## 5. Observation Context

Observation context is the surrounding information that makes an observation understandable within its limits.

Context may include:

- when the observation occurred;
- where or in what system it occurred;
- under which conditions;
- through which interaction;
- by which observer, system, instrument, or process;
- with which scope;
- with which sampling or temporal boundary;
- under which known limitations;
- under which assumptions relevant to understanding the observation.

These elements are not a mandatory schema. The necessary context depends on the risk that losing context would change interpretation, comparability, transfer, evidential use, traceability, or later revision.

Observation context prevents an observation from being detached from the conditions that made it meaningful.

## 6. Selection and Boundaries

Observation is selective.

Reality always contains more than can be observed or preserved. Attention selects. Instruments select. Sampling selects. Boundaries select. Recording systems select. Operational constraints select.

Selection does not make an observation invalid. It makes the observation bounded.

Selection should be recognized when it affects:

- what could have been noticed;
- what could not have been noticed;
- completeness;
- comparability;
- transfer to another context;
- later evidential use.

What was not observed must not be silently treated as what did not exist.

An observation preserves something about reality, not reality in its entirety.

## 7. Observation and Measurement

Measurement is a process, interaction, or instrument-mediated operation that may produce an observation.

Measurement is not automatically observation. A measurement becomes part of observation when its result is preserved or contextualized in a form useful to reasoning.

Observation is not limited to quantitative measurement. It may arise from qualitative experience, inspection, testimony, operational feedback, physical behavior, unexpected events, human perception, or instrument output.

Quantitative form does not automatically imply greater epistemic value. The value of an observation depends on its context, limits, provenance, fidelity to what was encountered, and later use in reasoning.

Measurement can make observation more precise for some purposes and misleading for others. The measurement scale, instrument, threshold, temporal window, and recording condition are part of the observation context when they affect meaning.

## 8. Observation and Interpretation

Observation and interpretation are distinguishable, but not always perfectly separable.

Description may contain categorization. Framing may guide attention. Selection may reflect prior assumptions. Instruments may encode assumptions about what counts as a detectable difference.

VAKI therefore does not pretend that observation is pure reality copied without mediation.

At the same time, stronger interpretive claims should remain distinguishable from what was perceived, measured, experienced, or detected. The observation should preserve enough structure for later reasoning to ask:

- What was actually encountered?
- What was selected or omitted?
- What context shaped the record?
- What meaning was inferred later?

Interpretation of evidential relevance belongs primarily to the Evidence Model. The Observation Model preserves the account that later interpretation may use, challenge, qualify, or revise.

## 9. Observation and Data

Data and observation are not synonyms.

Data may be recorded output from an interaction, measurement, perception, instrument, system, account, or experience. It may be raw, processed, structured, unstructured, quantitative, qualitative, contextualized, or context-poor.

Data becomes part of reasoning as observation when it preserves something perceived, measured, experienced, or detected with enough context to be understood as an account of an interaction with reality.

The same artifact may function as raw data in one context and as observation in another when its provenance, conditions, timing, process, limitations, and scope are sufficiently clear.

Human perception, operational experience, qualitative reports, physical traces, sensor output, machine-generated measurements, recorded events, and absence detected under defined conditions can all support observations. None is automatically evidence merely by being recorded.

## 10. Absence and Non-Observation

Absence of observation and observation of absence are different.

Absence of observation means no relevant observation was recorded, available, or made under conditions sufficient to notice the phenomenon.

Observation of absence means that absence itself was observed or detected under conditions where the phenomenon could meaningfully have been perceived, measured, experienced, or detected.

No observation was recorded does not automatically mean the phenomenon was absent.

An observation of absence requires enough context to understand:

- what was expected or detectable;
- under which conditions;
- through which process or instrument;
- within which scope or time window;
- with what known detection limits.

Whether such an observation bears on a claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision belongs to the Evidence Model.

## 11. Preservation

Preservation is the maintenance of enough observation content and context for the observation to remain inspectable.

Preservation allows an observation to be:

- inspected;
- compared;
- reconsidered;
- connected to later reasoning;
- corrected;
- challenged;
- reused within its valid scope.

Preservation does not require a specific file format, database, log, identifier, immutable store, event-sourcing model, schema, or version-control system.

Preservation does not imply infinite permanence. The value of preservation depends on the risk of losing meaning and the likelihood that the observation may matter for reasoning, learning, traceability, correction, or revision.

An unpreserved reaction, memory, output, or event may still have happened. It is weaker as a VAKI observation if later reasoning cannot inspect what was encountered and under which conditions.

## 12. Provenance

Provenance is the ability to understand where an observation came from and under which conditions it was produced or recorded.

Provenance may include origin, interaction, observer, instrument, process, operating condition, timing, scope, sampling, transformation, or limitation when those affect meaning.

The required provenance detail is proportional to the risk that, without it, the observation would be:

- misunderstood;
- overgeneralized;
- detached from conditions;
- incorrectly compared;
- treated as more reliable or transferable than justified.

This document does not define identity systems, metadata schemas, storage models, or provenance tooling.

The Evidence Model may use provenance to interpret evidential relevance, quality, and strength. The Observation Model owns the origin-side meaning of provenance.

## 13. Correction and Revision

Observations are revisable.

An observation may be:

- corrected;
- clarified;
- qualified;
- replaced by a better preserved account;
- recognized as distorted;
- recognized as incomplete;
- linked to newly discovered context.

Observation correction means that what was observed or preserved is corrected or better contextualized.

Evidence revision means that evidential meaning changes.

Model revision means that explanatory structure changes.

Decision revision means that commitment reasoning changes.

These revisions may influence one another, but they are not the same revision. Correcting an observation does not automatically prove that all reasoning based on the earlier observation was irrational at the time.

When an important observation is corrected, later reasoning should be able to discover where that observation affected evidence, decisions, commitments, or manifestations.

## 14. Observation and Feedback

Feedback becomes useful to VAKI reasoning through observation.

Feedback does not become observation merely because a reaction, response, consequence, or operational effect occurred. It becomes observation when something is perceived, measured, experienced, or detected and preserved with enough context to remain inspectable.

Feedback may arise from operation of machines, vessels, physical systems, research, markets, organizations, products, infrastructure, software, or other manifestations.

The Observation Model owns the boundary from feedback to preserved observation. The Evidence Model owns whether the resulting observation bears on a claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision.

## 15. Boundary with Signal and Evidence

Observation records what was perceived, measured, experienced, or detected.

Signal marks possible relevance.

Evidence exists when information bears on a claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision.

The relationship may be:

```text
Observation
  -> possible Signal
  -> Evidence relation
```

This is not a required workflow state sequence. An observation does not have to be formally labeled as signal before it can participate in evidence reasoning.

The Evidence Model remains the primary owner of Signal, Evidence, Evidence Strength, Body of Evidence, support, contradiction, qualification, inconclusive evidence, and state of uncertainty.

## 16. Observation Quality

Observation quality is multidimensional and context-dependent.

VAKI does not define a universal observation quality score. A single number would hide the reasons an observation may be useful for one purpose and weak for another.

Relevant dimensions may include:

- fidelity to what was encountered;
- completeness relative to purpose;
- context preservation;
- provenance clarity;
- repeatability where relevant;
- precision where relevant;
- timeliness where relevant;
- distortion risk.

Not every dimension matters in every context. Quantitative observation is not automatically superior to qualitative observation, and qualitative observation is not automatically less rigorous.

Evaluation of evidential strength belongs to the Evidence Model.

## 17. Traceability

Important observations should be traceable backward toward their origin and forward toward where they affected reasoning.

Origin-side trace:

```text
Observation
  -> provenance
  -> reality interaction, experiment, experience, operation, measurement, or detection context
```

Forward trace:

```text
Observation
  -> evidential relation
  -> affected claim, uncertainty, model, hypothesis, or decision context
  -> informed Decision
  -> Commitment
  -> Manifestation
```

This traceability is conceptual. It does not define graph schemas, identifiers, databases, storage systems, or tooling.

Responsibility is layered:

- Observation Model owns origin-side observation trace.
- Evidence Model owns evidential relationship trace.
- Decision Model owns reasoning and commitment trace.

## 18. Observation Failure Modes

Common observation failure modes include:

- interpretation mistaken for observation;
- observation mistaken for complete reality;
- measurement mistaken for neutral truth;
- context loss;
- provenance loss;
- sampling blindness;
- selection blindness;
- observer effect ignored where relevant;
- instrument limitations ignored;
- absence of observation mistaken for observation of absence;
- preserved reaction mistaken for useful observation;
- observation corrected without tracing downstream impact;
- observation generalized beyond scope;
- temporal context ignored;
- aggregation hiding relevant variation;
- qualitative observation dismissed merely because it is non-quantitative;
- quantitative observation overtrusted merely because it is numerical.

These failure modes are not a checklist or workflow. They are recurring ways observation can become detached from reality, context, limits, or later reasoning.

## 19. Invariants

Across VAKI manifestations:

1. Reality exceeds every observation of it.
2. Observation is reality-constrained but always selective and bounded.
3. Observation is not automatically evidence.
4. Observation is not equivalent to interpretation, though perfect separation may not always be possible.
5. What was observed should remain distinguishable, as far as practicable, from what was inferred from it.
6. Measurement can produce observations but does not exhaust the meaning of observation.
7. Quantitative form does not automatically imply greater epistemic value.
8. Absence of observation is not automatically observation of absence.
9. Observation context matters when losing it would change interpretation, comparability, transfer, or evidential use.
10. Provenance should be preserved proportionally to the consequence of misunderstanding an observation.
11. Observation can be corrected without implying that all reasoning based on it was irrational at the time.
12. Observation preservation exists to support inspection, comparison, learning, traceability, and revision.
13. Feedback becomes useful to reasoning through observation, not merely through reaction.
14. Observation quality is multidimensional and context-dependent.
15. No observation should be treated as a complete representation of reality.
16. Important observations should be traceable backward to origin context and forward to where they affected reasoning.
17. Observation Model does not determine evidential relevance or decision strength.
18. Manifestations may implement observation differently but must not silently redefine its core semantics.

## 20. Relationship to Existing Foundation Documents

This model refines the Observation layer of the Thinking Model without duplicating the full Thinking Model.

It supports the Evidence Model by owning observation semantics. The Evidence Model owns when and how observations or other relevant information bear on claims, hypotheses, models, uncertainties, or decisions. Future Evidence Model revisions should be able to reference this model instead of carrying deep observation definitions, while retaining necessary boundary language.

It supports the Decision Model by preserving the observation-side trace that later evidence and decision reasoning may depend on. The Observation Model does not define decision reasoning, commitment, reversibility, optionality, cost of delay, or learning value.

It supports the Design Methodology by clarifying what it means to observe before abstracting. Manifestations may implement observation practices differently, but they should preserve these semantics.

It refines the Principles by making explicit:

- Reality over narrative;
- Make assumptions explicit;
- Evidence over intuition, intuition as hypothesis;
- Experiment to learn;
- Documentation preserves meaning;
- Evolution is part of design.

It respects ADR-0001 by remaining inside the Foundation semantic boundary. It defines shared meaning for observation but does not prescribe local implementation mechanisms, tools, operational procedures, schemas, or governance.
