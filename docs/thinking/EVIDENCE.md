# Evidence Model

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the epistemic model VAKI uses to reason from reality toward knowledge and decisions.

Its purpose is to make evidence precise without turning it into a rigid workflow, a storage schema, or a domain-specific method.

VAKI treats evidence as contextual, relational, revisable information that can change the credibility of a claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision.

The model exists to preserve autonomy by making visible:

- what is known;
- how it is known;
- what remains uncertain;
- where interpretation begins;
- why a decision was reasonable in its original context.

## 2. Position in the Thinking Model

In the VAKI Thinking Model, evidence sits between reality-facing observation and decision reasoning:

```text
Reality
  -> interaction with reality
  -> Observation
  -> possible Signal
  -> Evidence relation
  -> changed state of uncertainty
  -> Decision Reasoning
  -> Manifestation
```

This position does not mean evidence is produced only by formal experiments.

Evidence may arise from observation, operational feedback, experience, comparison, deliberate experiment, or other reality-facing interactions. What matters is not the source category, but whether the information is related to something under consideration, such as a question, claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision context, with enough context to be interpreted responsibly.

Feedback does not become evidence merely because it occurred. The Observation Model owns the feedback-to-observation boundary; the Evidence Model owns whether the resulting observation or other relevant information bears evidentially on something under consideration.

Evidence is a boundary layer. It connects reality-facing observation to decision-facing interpretation without owning the formation of observations or allowing decisions to mechanically follow from evidence.

## 3. Epistemic Layers

VAKI distinguishes the following layers:

```text
Reality
  -> interaction, measurement, or experience
Data
  -> possible Observation role
Observation
  -> relevance detection
Signal
  -> relation to a claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision
Evidence
  -> interpretation and synthesis
Knowledge
  -> application under uncertainty
Decision
```

This sequence is not a mandatory process. It is a set of distinctions. It describes common epistemic roles, not a required pipeline through which every record must pass.

The same artifact may participate in several layers, but the layers must not be treated as synonyms. A measurement is not automatically an observation. An observation is not automatically a signal. A signal is not automatically evidence. Evidence is not automatically knowledge. Knowledge does not remove uncertainty. A decision is not proof.

Observation semantics are defined in the [Observation Model](OBSERVATION_MODEL.md). This document retains only the observation boundary language needed to explain how observations may participate in evidence reasoning.

Interpretation is shown after evidence because synthesized evidence must be interpreted before it can become knowledge or support decision reasoning. It is also active earlier: interpretation helps determine whether information bears on an object of evaluation at all. Evidence and interpretation therefore form an iterative relationship, while remaining conceptually distinct.

## 4. Reality

Reality is the external reference that constrains VAKI reasoning.

Reality is not fully captured by any data, observation, model, or decision. It exceeds representation. VAKI therefore treats every representation as partial, situated, and revisable.

When evidence contradicts a model, the model is provisional. Reality is not.

## 5. Data

Data is recorded output from an interaction, measurement, perception, instrument, system, account, or experience.

Data may be structured or unstructured, quantitative or qualitative, direct or indirect. Data can be useful, misleading, incomplete, duplicated, corrupted, stale, biased, or irrelevant.

The distinction between data and observation is conceptual and role-based, not a mandatory processing pipeline.

Data is not automatically observation and is not automatically evidence.

The same artifact may function as raw data in one reasoning context, as part of an observation in another, and as evidence in another only when it bears on something under consideration.

The transition from data to observation depends on whether the record preserves something perceived, measured, experienced, or detected with enough context to be understood as an account of interaction with reality. That boundary is defined by the [Observation Model](OBSERVATION_MODEL.md).

Data without context can still be retained, but its evidential value is limited until its source, scope, and limits can be responsibly interpreted.

## 6. Observation

Evidence may use observations as one possible source, but this document does not define observation semantics.

Observation is not yet explanation. Observation is not automatically signal or evidence. Measurement is not automatically evidence.

Observation semantics, including context, selection, preservation, provenance origin, measurement boundary, limits, absence/non-observation, feedback-to-observation, and correction, are defined by the [Observation Model](OBSERVATION_MODEL.md).

Evidence interpretation must respect observation context, provenance, limits, and scope, but those qualities do not by themselves determine evidential meaning.

## 7. Signal

A signal is an observed pattern, difference, event, absence, change, or indicator that may contain information relevant to a question.

Signal is a relevance layer. It marks that something in an observation may matter.

A signal is not automatically evidence because relevance is still provisional. It may be noise, coincidence, artifact of measurement, proxy behavior, local effect, or a pattern that matters only under certain assumptions.

An observation does not need to pass through a mandatory signal state before participating in an evidence relation.

Signal should therefore remain connected to:

- the observation from which it was detected;
- the question or uncertainty that made it relevant;
- possible alternative explanations;
- known ways the signal could be misleading.

VAKI preserves the distinction between signal and outcome. A signal can inform reasoning without being treated as final success.

## 8. Evidence

Evidence is information that changes the credibility of a claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision.

Evidence is relational. Nothing is evidence in the abstract.

Something becomes evidence only when it is interpreted as bearing on at least one object of evaluation, such as:

- a claim;
- a hypothesis;
- a model;
- an uncertainty;
- a decision context.

The same observation may be strong evidence for one claim, weak evidence for another, irrelevant to a third, and misleading for a fourth.

This does not make evidence arbitrary. The interpretation that establishes evidential relevance must itself be constrained by provenance, conditions, measurement quality, scope, alternatives, and known limitations.

Evidence should make visible:

- what it bears on;
- whether it supports, weakens, contradicts, qualifies, or fails to resolve that object;
- the context in which it was produced;
- the assumptions required for it to matter;
- its known limitations.

Evidence does not eliminate interpretation. It constrains interpretation, and interpretation clarifies what the evidence is evidence of.

## 9. Interpretation

Interpretation is the act of assigning meaning to information and evidence in context.

This section concerns evidential interpretation: how information bears on a claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision context. Observation-side selection, mediation, description, and the description/inference boundary are owned by the [Observation Model](OBSERVATION_MODEL.md).

Interpretation helps evaluate whether and how an observation, signal, result, or absence bears on an object of evaluation. It may connect multiple pieces of evidence, compare competing explanations, identify limitations, synthesize a body of evidence, or revise a model. It should not be hidden inside the evidence itself.

Evidence and interpretation are iterative:

- interpretation may establish or revise the evidential significance of an observation;
- evidence constrains which interpretations remain credible;
- multiple pieces of evidence may require synthesis before their combined meaning is clear;
- interpretation may change while the underlying observation remains unchanged;
- a revised interpretation may change whether an observation supports, weakens, qualifies, or fails to resolve a claim.

Responsible interpretation considers:

- whether the evidence actually relates to the claim or decision at hand;
- whether it concerns the real objective or only a proxy;
- whether alternative explanations remain plausible;
- whether the evidence is local, systemic, temporary, or transferable;
- whether the result depends on fragile assumptions;
- whether relevant uncertainty remains unresolved.

Interpretation must preserve uncertainty where certainty is not justified.

## 10. Body of Evidence

A body of evidence is a set of relevant evidence considered together in relation to a claim, uncertainty, model, or decision.

The concept is lightweight. It does not imply a package, dossier, approval bundle, aggregation procedure, score, schema, or mandatory format.

A body of evidence may contain:

- supporting evidence;
- contradicting evidence;
- qualifying evidence;
- unresolved contradictions;
- known gaps;
- dependencies between apparently separate evidence.

Important decisions often depend on a body of evidence rather than a single evidential relationship. The body matters because individual pieces can interact: several weak but independent pieces may become more meaningful together, while several apparently separate pieces may collapse into one dependency when they share a source, assumption, or measurement process.

## 11. Knowledge

Knowledge is structured understanding that can support explanation, prediction, decision, action, maintenance, or adaptation.

Knowledge is not a pile of evidence. It is evidence, often from a body of evidence, interpreted, organized, bounded, and made reusable.

Knowledge should preserve:

- what is understood;
- where it applies;
- where it may fail;
- which evidence supports it;
- which uncertainty remains;
- which assumptions it depends on;
- what would require revision.

Knowledge remains provisional. Its value in VAKI is measured by whether it increases the capacity to understand, decide, act, maintain, repair, adapt, and learn.

## 12. Uncertainty

Uncertainty is the recognized limit of current knowledge, observation, model, or interpretation.

Uncertainty is not ignorance in general. It is a specific boundary around what cannot yet be known, inferred, predicted, explained, or decided with the desired reliability.

VAKI treats uncertainty as design material. It should be made explicit when it affects important reasoning.

Uncertainty may concern:

- what is true;
- what matters;
- whether observations are reliable;
- whether a model applies;
- whether evidence transfers across contexts;
- what consequences may follow;
- whether a decision can be reversed;
- what will change over time.

Evidence may reduce uncertainty, reveal new uncertainty, relocate uncertainty, or show that an earlier uncertainty was framed incorrectly.

The role of evidence is not to produce certainty. Its role is to change the state of uncertainty enough to improve reasoning.

The Evidence Model owns uncertainty semantics and how evidence changes the state of uncertainty. The Decision Model uses the current state of uncertainty as an input to decision reasoning without redefining it.

One useful conceptual bridge is:

```text
Experiment or interaction with reality
  -> Observation
  -> Evidence
  -> changed state of uncertainty
  -> decision reasoning
```

This bridge is not a workflow. It names the epistemic movement that the Decision Model uses without defining decision reasoning here.

## 13. Evidence Quality

Evidence quality is multidimensional.

VAKI does not define a universal confidence score. A single number can hide the reasons evidence is strong in one respect and weak in another.

Evidence quality should be judged through dimensions appropriate to the case. The following dimensions are domain-independent enough to guide Foundation reasoning:

- Relevance: whether the evidence bears on the actual claim, hypothesis, uncertainty, model, or decision.
- Reliability: whether the source, process, instrument, actor, or record can be trusted for this purpose.
- Validity: whether the evidence measures, indicates, or reflects what it is being used to claim.
- Independence: whether the evidence is genuinely separate from other evidence or merely repeats the same source, assumption, process, or bias.
- Coverage: whether the evidence covers enough of the relevant conditions, cases, actors, time, or system behavior.
- Timeliness: whether the evidence is current enough for the context being reasoned about.
- Repeatability: whether similar interaction under materially similar conditions can produce comparable observations, where repeatability is meaningful.
- Transferability: whether evidence from one context can responsibly inform another.
- Sensitivity to assumptions: whether the interpretation changes significantly when assumptions change.

Not every dimension applies equally in every domain. The invariant is not the checklist. The invariant is that evidence quality cannot be collapsed into unexamined confidence.

Observation quality may influence evidence quality, but it does not determine evidence strength by itself. A precise, well-preserved observation can be weak evidence for a broad claim, while an imperfect observation may still have evidential relevance within appropriate limits.

## 14. Supporting, Contradicting, Absent, and Inconclusive Evidence

Evidence may affect an object of evaluation in different ways.

Supporting evidence increases the credibility of a claim, hypothesis, model, interpretation, or decision under stated conditions.

Contradicting evidence decreases credibility or shows that the object fails under some condition.

Qualifying evidence narrows scope, reveals boundary conditions, or shows that a claim is true only under more specific circumstances than originally believed.

Null evidence is an expected observation that did not occur under conditions where it should have been detectable.

The [Observation Model](OBSERVATION_MODEL.md) owns the observation-side distinction between absence of observation and observation of absence. Evidence Model owns whether an observed absence bears evidentially on a claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision context.

Three layers should remain distinct:

- no relevant observation exists;
- an observation records absence under meaningful detection conditions;
- that observation bears evidentially on something under consideration.

An inconclusive result means the available evidence does not responsibly support, weaken, or resolve the object of evaluation.

VAKI rejects the simplistic rule that absence of evidence is evidence of absence.

Non-observation becomes informative only when:

- an observation was expected;
- the observation process was capable of detecting it;
- the relevant conditions were present;
- the detection limits are understood;
- alternative explanations for non-detection are considered.

## 15. Contradictory Evidence

Contradictory evidence should not be forced into artificial agreement.

Conflicting evidence may mean:

- one observation or interpretation is wrong;
- each piece applies in a different context;
- the model is incomplete;
- hidden assumptions differ;
- measurement processes are not comparable;
- the system changed over time;
- a real tension exists that has not yet been resolved.

VAKI permits:

- unresolved contradiction;
- competing explanations;
- context partitioning;
- model revision;
- further observation or experimentation;
- decision under unresolved uncertainty.

The goal is not to erase contradiction. The goal is to preserve enough structure for responsible reasoning.

## 16. Negative Knowledge

Negative knowledge is preserved understanding of what did not work, what was invalidated, where a model failed, or which boundary condition matters.

Negative knowledge includes:

- failed approaches with their context;
- invalidated hypotheses;
- known failure modes;
- misleading signals;
- discarded alternatives with preserved reasoning;
- conditions under which an otherwise useful model does not apply.

Negative knowledge is valuable when it prevents repeated failure, clarifies boundaries, or improves future judgment.

It should not be reduced to failure as outcome. A failed approach can be successful learning if it changes what VAKI understands and prevents the same lesson from being paid for again.

## 17. Evidence Lifetime and Contextual Validity

Evidence has a validity horizon.

The horizon is not a fixed time-to-live. It is the range of conditions under which the evidence remains meaningful.

Evidence may lose or change validity when:

- the system changes;
- the context changes;
- the measurement process changes;
- constraints change;
- objectives change;
- actors or usage patterns change;
- new information appears;
- earlier assumptions are revised.

Evidence should therefore remain connected to the conditions under which it was produced and interpreted.

Observation Context is origin-side: it explains the conditions needed to understand what was observed and with what limits, as defined by the [Observation Model](OBSERVATION_MODEL.md).

Contextual Validity is evidence-side: it describes the scope and conditions under which an evidential interpretation remains applicable.

Old evidence is not automatically invalid. Recent evidence is not automatically better. Validity depends on whether the evidence still bears on the current question under current conditions.

## 18. Traceability

Important decisions should be traceable back to the evidence and observations that made them understandable.

Evidence relationship trace is:

```text
Evidence
  -> Observation or other relevant source
  -> relationship to claim, hypothesis, model, uncertainty, or decision context
```

Decision reasoning connects upward through:

```text
Decision
  -> Body of Evidence
```

Origin-side observation trace belongs to the [Observation Model](OBSERVATION_MODEL.md), including provenance from observation toward reality interaction, experiment, experience, operation, measurement, or detection context.

Decision reasoning and commitment trace belong to the [Decision Model](../method/DECISION_MODEL.md).

Traceability is not bureaucracy. It is the preservation of reasoning so that a future person can understand, challenge, revise, or reuse the decision.

At this stage, traceability is conceptual. This document does not define a database, schema, template, identifier system, or tool.

## 19. Revision and Supersession

Evidence, interpretation, and knowledge are revisable.

New evidence may modify an interpretation. A revised interpretation may modify knowledge. Revised knowledge may change what decision is appropriate.

Observation correction is distinct from evidence revision. Observation correction changes or better contextualizes what was observed or preserved. Evidence revision changes evidential meaning, relationship, or applicability.

Revision does not imply that an earlier decision was irrational.

A prior decision can remain understandable and rational if it was made from the evidence, uncertainty, constraints, and context available at the time. Later evidence may supersede it without making the original reasoning dishonest or careless.

VAKI must therefore preserve enough context to avoid hindsight bias.

Supersession should distinguish:

- what changed in reality;
- what changed in observation;
- what changed in interpretation;
- what changed in knowledge;
- what changed in constraints or objectives;
- what was unknown but relevant at the time.

The architecture should allow observation correction to cause affected evidence relationships to be reconsidered, which may change a body of evidence, the state of uncertainty, and possibly decision reasoning. This is a conceptual dependency, not a required workflow.

## 20. Boundary with Decision Model

Evidence strength is not decision strength.

Strong evidence for a narrow claim may be insufficient for a broad decision. Weak evidence may still require action when waiting is costly, the decision is reversible, or inaction creates greater risk.

The level and kind of evidence needed for a decision depends on factors such as:

- consequence of error;
- reversibility;
- cost of waiting;
- availability of further observation or experiment;
- uncertainty that remains after the evidence is interpreted;
- scope of the decision;
- whether the decision creates or removes future options.

This document defines the epistemic boundary needed by decisions. It does not define the full VAKI Decision Model.

The Decision Model defines how decisions use a changed state of uncertainty, body of evidence, trade-offs, reversibility, timing, and consequences to choose an appropriate commitment.

## 21. Invariants

Across VAKI manifestations:

1. Reality is the external reference; representations remain provisional.
2. Data, observation, signal, evidence, interpretation, knowledge, uncertainty, and decision must remain distinguishable even when they interact.
3. Evidence is relational, not absolute.
4. Evidence is contextual; provenance, conditions, timing, process, scope, uncertainty, and limitations matter.
5. Evidence and interpretation are iterative: interpretation establishes evidential significance, while evidence constrains interpretation.
6. Evidence quality is multidimensional and must not be hidden inside a universal score.
7. Evidence can support, contradict, qualify, fail to resolve, or become informative through justified non-observation.
8. A body of evidence may contain support, contradiction, qualification, gaps, and dependencies.
9. Contradictory evidence may remain unresolved when the current model cannot responsibly resolve it.
10. Negative knowledge should be preserved when it prevents repeated error or clarifies boundaries.
11. Evidence has a contextual validity horizon and may require revision.
12. Important decisions should be traceable to evidence and observations.
13. Later supersession must not erase the rationality of earlier decisions made under different evidence and constraints.

## 22. Relationship to Existing Foundation Documents

This model extends the Thinking Model by formalizing the evidence layer without changing the core loop.

It supports the Design Methodology by clarifying how observations, hypotheses, experiments, evidence, interpretation, and decisions remain connected while preserving uncertainty.

It refines the Principles by making explicit:

- Reality over narrative;
- Evidence over intuition, intuition as hypothesis;
- Preserve negative knowledge;
- Distinguish signal from outcome;
- Reversibility where uncertainty is high;
- Documentation preserves meaning.

It respects ADR-0001 by remaining domain-independent. It defines shared meaning for the Foundation but does not prescribe tools, schemas, workflows, technologies, operational procedures, or local governance.

Manifestations may implement this model differently, but they should not silently redefine its core distinctions.
