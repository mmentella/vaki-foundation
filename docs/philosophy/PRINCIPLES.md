# VAKI Principles

The principles define how VAKI approaches knowledge, design, engineering, and evolution across domains.

## 1. Autonomy over dependency

Prefer systems that increase the ability to understand, decide, operate, maintain, repair, and adapt.

Avoid unnecessary dependence on opaque tools, undocumented knowledge, or irreplaceable actors.

## 2. Reality over narrative

Models, plans, and beliefs are provisional.

Reality has precedence. When evidence contradicts a model, revise the model.

## 3. Questions before solutions

Do not begin with a preferred technology, architecture, or implementation.

Frame the problem before choosing the solution space.

## 4. Make assumptions explicit

Hidden assumptions are unmanaged risks.

Important assumptions should be visible, testable where possible, and revisited when conditions change.

## 5. Evidence over intuition, intuition as hypothesis

Intuition is useful for generating hypotheses, not for granting them truth.

Important claims should be supported by evidence proportional to the consequences of being wrong.

## 6. Experiment to learn

Experiments are a way to learn through deliberate reality-facing interaction.

A good experiment has a clear inquiry relation, explicit assumptions, observable conditions, and the possibility of changing understanding or future action.

## 7. Preserve negative knowledge

Failed approaches, invalidated hypotheses, and dead ends are part of the knowledge base when their context and evidence are preserved.

Do not repeatedly pay for the same lesson.

## 8. Distinguish signal from outcome

A proxy, metric, benchmark, prediction, or local optimization is not automatically the final objective.

Success at one layer must not be confused with system-level success.

## 9. Design for observability

A system should expose enough state to understand what it is doing, why it is doing it, and where it is failing.

Unobservable systems are difficult to improve and easy to mythologize.

## 10. Simplicity through understanding

Do not hide complexity and call the result simple.

Reduce complexity by understanding structure, eliminating unnecessary elements, and clarifying boundaries.

## 11. Reversibility where uncertainty is high

When uncertainty is high, prefer decisions that are cheap to reverse, easy to inspect, and capable of producing information.

Increase commitment as evidence increases.

## 12. Separation of foundation and manifestation

The Foundation defines invariant philosophy and method.

Manifestations interpret and implement those ideas within a domain.

A manifestation may extend the Foundation locally, but it must not silently redefine foundational meaning.

## 13. Local freedom, explicit boundaries

Different manifestations may choose different technologies, architectures, processes, and tools.

Shared principles should create coherence without forcing accidental uniformity.

## 14. Documentation preserves meaning

Document decisions, assumptions, boundaries, and learned knowledge where losing them would force rediscovery.

Documentation should serve understanding and evolution, not bureaucracy.

## 15. Evolution is part of design

Systems exist in changing environments.

Design not only for initial construction, but also for operation, feedback, correction, adaptation, and retirement.
